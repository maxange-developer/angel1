I didn't tell anyone about the hearing loss for the first three years.

Not my parents, not my teachers, not the handful of friends I had at fourteen. I just got better at reading lips, positioning myself near windows, asking people to repeat things in a way that made it sound like I was distracted rather than deaf.

It worked longer than it should have.

## The quiet years

By 2015 I'd lost about 60% of hearing in both ears. The audiologist had a name for it — bilateral sensorineural hearing loss — and a prognosis that amounted to: *we can slow it, we can't stop it*.

What she didn't say was that I'd spend the next decade developing a kind of hyper-focused attention to compensate. When you can't hear ambient sound, you get very good at reading context. You watch faces. You read body language. You notice the things people mean but don't say.

You also spend a lot of time alone, because social situations are exhausting when every conversation is a lip-reading exercise.

I found code during this period. Not as a career plan — I was fifteen, career plans were abstract — but as a thing I could do in silence. HTML rendered without sound. JavaScript executed without sound. The terminal responded to me, not the other way around.

> The screen didn't care if I couldn't hear it. That was the whole point.

## What silence teaches

There's a specific quality of focus that comes from isolation. I don't romanticize it — twelve years of partial deafness is not a superpower, it's a disability, and the social cost was real and ongoing.

But the work habits that came out of it were something. I learned to work in long, unbroken stretches. I learned to be comfortable with ambiguity. I learned that most meetings are just documents waiting to be written.

I also learned something about building alone. When you're deaf, you don't get the casual team communication that normally cements a codebase — the quick Slack message, the overheard conversation, the hallway sync. You work from the spec, from the documentation, from what you can read on the screen. You become very good at making decisions from first principles because you don't have the luxury of consensus.

This is, I'd later learn, exactly how good product engineering works. You make a call, you ship it, you measure, you iterate. The absence of unnecessary communication is a feature.

## The surgery

In 2024, a surgeon at Ancona's regional hospital offered me an option I'd been told wasn't available: a procedure to repair the nerve damage. Success rate somewhere around 30%. Recovery time uncertain. He was honest about the odds.

I said yes immediately.

The weeks after the surgery were strange. Sounds came back unevenly — certain frequencies first, then others. The first time I heard rain properly I stood at the window for twenty minutes. I'd heard rain before, obviously, but filtered through years of loss. This was different.

I spent about a month recalibrating. Meetings felt different. Music felt different. The city I'd lived in for years sounded like a place I'd never been.

## Now

I'm writing this from Tenerife, where I moved in October 2025. The trade winds are audible. The ocean is audible. The coffee machine at the cafe downstairs is, some mornings, very audible.

I build AI products. I do it alone, for fixed prices, for founders who need something built and don't want twelve meetings to figure out what. The twelve silent years were good training for this.

The focus was always there. The hearing was optional.
